* R1: "I found somewhat lacking is an extended reflection on these results"
  R2: "A more explicit bird-eye view connecting these aspects ... would further strengthen the contribution"
  R3: "the broader theoretical implications of these observations remain unclear"

We agree with the reviewers that the paper currently does not include an extended reflection, or bird-eye view, on the connection between the structure of Maehara interpolants, cut admissibility and equality of proofs in substructural logics. But we believe that such a perspective can be given using the language of category theory, in particular employing the multicategories of Lambek. Multicategory have morphisms with a list of objects as domain, not necessarily a single object, and as such have strong connections to sequent calculi for variants of intuitionistic logic.

In multicategorical models of sequent calculus, the cut rule is interpreted as morphism composition and interpolant triples would be interpreted as certain factorizations of morphisms. In this setting, studying well-definedness and equivalence of interpolation triples translates to understanding the structure of such factorizations. After submission, we realized that this perspective is similar to the one in Pavlović's paper "Categorical interpolation: Descent and the Beck-Chevalley condition without direct images", but for multicategories instead of categories.

We plan to undertake this multicategorical investigation of interpolation in future work, but we strongly believe that the results described in our paper, which are of syntactic, proof-theoretic nature, will be crucial for unraveling the more general and illuminating categorical story. We will add a paragraph on this perspective in the introductory section, where we will also describe the relationship of our work with the other (few) attempts at a category-theoretic understanding of Craig interpolation (Pavlović, Čubrić, Pitts, Makkai).

* R2: "why the technical developments in the paper are of particular interest to the logic and automated reasoning community"

The study of well-defined Maehara interpolation, and in particular the structure of interpolant triples and their equivalence, is important for understanding how differences in proofs (differences that preserve proof denotations, i.e. that result in equivalent proofs) translates to differences in the corresponding interpolants computed by Maehara's method.

Being able to quantify this difference can be useful in applications. For example, in program verification. Model checkers based on interpolation produce loop invariants by iteratively finding a proof of a certain (un)reachibility condition and then applying an interpolation algorithm (such as Maehara's method) on this proof. Our work, and its extension to more expressive logical systems and different interpolation algorithms, could potentially be used for the analysis of these model checkers, for understanding how the modification of the proof search procedure translates to the production of different loop invariants.

We also conjecture possible uses of well-defined interpolation for the efficient computation of interpolants. For example, given equivalent proofs f ≗ g and an interpolant D for f produced by Maehara's algorithm, then we should be able to compute an interpolant for g, equivalent to D, directly by inspecting the given equality of proofs, without the need of running Maehara's method also on g. This could be efficient if the differences between f and g are small and located towards the lower parts of the proof trees.

We will add a discussion on these ideas and conjectures to the conclusive section of the paper.

* R1: "I do not see why a chain of this particular shape [zig-zag] is forced by the definition"

The zig-zag is necessary because equivalent derivations need not produce interpolant formulae that are connected by a simple, directed chain of derivations. In particular, there are equivalent derivations whose interpolants do not imply each other, but can imply or be implied by the interpolant extracted from a third equivalent derivation.

For example, we found two equivalent derivations f and f', to which Maehara's method assign interpolant formulae ((D₀ ⇒ E₀) ⊗ F₀) ⊗ ((D₁ ⊗ F₁) ⇐ E₁) and (D₀ ⇒ (E₀ ⊗ F₀)) ⊗ (D₁ ⊗ (F₁ ⇐ E₁)), respectively. These formulae do not imply each other. However, they are both implied by ((D₀ ⇒ E₀) ⊗ F₀) ⊗ ((D₁ ⊗ F₁) ⇐ E₁), which is the interpolant formula of another derivation f'' equivalent to both f and f'.

We will add this concrete example motivating the need of zig-zags after Definition 1.
